Andy Li

BitterCoin: The Worst Miner Ever

What is BitterCoin?

  • An installation artwork of a old calculator machine hacked to function as a Bitcoin miner.
  • Validates pending Bitcoin transactions in the Blockchain (an online distributed database).
  • Mining operations are displayed on the calculator's screen and printed.
  • Combines Internet of Things (IoT), media archaeology, and economic commentary.

What is Bitcoin mining?

  • Bitcoin mining is like a huge computer race. Lots of computers try to guess a special number to get bitcoins as rewards.
  • What is Hashrate? Hashrate shows how fast all the miners are guessing. Each try is a “hash.” A higher hashrate means more guesses per second. More hashrate = stronger network = header to guess = more energy to use
Source: Blockchain.com and YCharts

Theme 1: Market Structure

Economic Concepts

Types of Market Structures

  • Perfect Competition: many firms, identical products, price takers
  • Oligopoly: few large firms, strategic interdependence
  • Monopoly: single seller, unique product, price maker

Key Concepts

  • Barriers to Entry: capital requirements, economies of scale, regulation
  • Information Asymmetry: unequal knowledge between buyers and sellers

Conceptual Analysis

  • By design, anyone can start mining → perfect-competition model where rewards cover costs and profits vanish.
  • Over time, expensive ASIC hardware, high electricity bills & technical know-how limit newcomers.
  • Large operations predict difficulty changes & negotiate power deals—small miners lack this info.
  • Result: hash-power pools around a few firms, shifting the market toward an oligopoly.

Empirical Analysis: Mining Concentration

  • Data shows high concentration. Top 0.1% miners control ~50% capacity.
  • Top mining pools (aggregators of hash power) dominate, supporting the oligopolistic view. This means the "free entry" leads to a competitive race won by few, large, efficient players.
  • Caveat: the data is for a global stage, market structures in different countries could differ: more firms in the USA means more competition. For cetrain countries, Khazakstan, may see more intensed Oligopoly with less competitors.
Source: Makarov and Schoar, Blockchain Analysis of the Bitcoin Market.

Empirical Analysis: Miner Profitability & Costs

  • The chart shows average daily earnings per unit of hash power versus estimated running costs.
  • Spikes in revenue lure new miners into the network, which raises competition and drives up total costs.
  • As more miners join, the gap between revenue and cost narrows, squeezing profit margins toward zero.
  • This behavior mirrors Ma, Gans & Tourky’s theory: free entry pushes resource costs up until profits are competed away.
Source: Coin Metrics, Bitcoin Miners' Income Stabilizes Post-Halving.

Theme 2: Tragedy of the Commons

Economic Concepts

What is Tragedy of the Commons?

  • When many people share a resource—like fish in a lake—unlimited use can exhaust it.
  • Each person gains the full benefit of extra use, but everyone bears the loss when the resource is depleted.
  • Without clear rules or limits, shared resources are quickly overused.
  • Small communities can protect a commons by agreeing on limits and penalties, but that requires trust and effective leadership.

Conceptual Analysis

  • Individual incentive: Each miner boosts electricity use for more Bitcoin, while environmental damage (pollution, e-waste) is shared by all.
  • Collective harm: Unchecked, this competitive mining strains power grids and increases overall emissions.

Nash Equilibrium & Miner's Dilemma: In this "Miner's Dilemma" on the shared 'energy commons,' if all miners act selfishly to maximize profit, a stable state (Nash Equilibrium) emerges. No single miner benefits by changing strategy alone, leading to collective energy overuse and environmental harm, even if cooperation were better. The table illustrates this.

Empirical Analysis: Incentive Drives Overconsumption

  • As Bitcoin prices rise, miners ramp up hashrate and energy use to boost profits.
  • This self-interested response increases network-wide electricity demand.
  • Shared energy resources are overdrawn—no miner wants to fall behind.
  • The result mirrors a Nash equilibrium: rational for individuals, harmful for the group.
Guidi et al. (2025) Guidi et al., The Environmental Burden of the United States’ Bitcoin Mining Boom.

Empirical Analysis: Policy & Shifting Incentives

  • To solve mining’s externalities, policies must realign incentives.
  • Pigovian taxes (like the proposed DAME tax) price in energy and emissions costs.
  • Miners then face the true social cost of overconsumption.
  • This shifts outcomes away from overuse and toward collective sustainability.
  • Caveat: The data a geneerated from predictions with assumptions like miner behavior, tax enforcement, and energy substitution, may not hold in practice.
Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance (2022) CCAF, Cambridge Bitcoin Electricity Consumption Index.

'BitterCoin': Economic Concepts Embodied

The 'BitterCoin' artwork, a deliberately slow "miner," makes Bitcoin's complex economics easier to grasp by showing:

  • Market Realities: Its simple design highlights how high costs (barriers to entry) in real Bitcoin mining limit competition, favoring a few large players (an oligopoly) rather than an open market for everyone.
  • Resource Use: The artwork's pointless energy consumption mirrors the 'Tragedy of the Commons,' where individuals use vast shared energy for private profit, risking environmental harm from overuse and pollution.
The "BitterCoin" installation by César Escudero Andaluz & Martín Nadal (2016).
